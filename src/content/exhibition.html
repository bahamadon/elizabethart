Elizabeth Heth, a native Edmontonian, discovered a greater connection with history through the passing of the city centre airport into the province's history. During the time in which there was some contention whether the airport would be closed, she found that she was receiving information from both sides and was intrigued by the passion and vitriol.

To explore the issue she found contacts who related stories regarding the airport, and the historical significance. In standing on what had once been commonly known as Blatchford field there seemed to be an interrupt, and a suspension, in the significance of human history. The aircraft were characters onto themselves with a strong similarity to their shorter-lived human counterparts, requiring air, fuel, care and purpose.

In the collection of these histories and complexities, Elizabeth found she was approaching these paintings less as snapshot of history and more as portraits, finding character and story in architecture as lovely as the familiar human form.
